AnyOprSetForceDepKin¶

Type

Abstract

Parent class

AnyOprSetMechSolver

Description¶

This folder-class holds parameters used for coupled inverse dynamics and force-dependent kinematics analysis.

Force dependent kinematics essentially implies that the system is kinematically indeterminate, i.e., the system has less kinematical motion drivers than degrees of freedom. Under general circumstances this would call for a forward dynamic solver solving the differential equations over time; however, under special mechanical circumstances the kinematic force dependency is not influencing the gross motion of the system significantly and for such cases AnyBody’s inverse dynamic solvers is extended with a solver that couples the inverse dynamics solver with a quasi-static analysis of selected degrees of freedom.

The selected degrees of freedom are kinematic constraints (see class AnyKinEq) with type specification (CType) set to “force-dependent”. These degrees of freedom will be assumed to be static in all time steps and solved accordingly. “Static” implies that velocities and accelerations are set to zero and the solver will attempt to find a position where the residual force in that direction of motion vanishes. This is only possible if the mechanical system, in particular the degrees of freedom in question, are sufficiently supported by elatic structure to provide a stationary position with minimal potential energy.

Moreover, the “static” assumption must hold for the given analysis and it is up to the user to evaluate this requirement. Generally, this is aimed at degrees of freedom with very small motion compared to the gross motion of the system for instance due to relatively stiff supporting structures. In other word, this is aimed for the “borderland” between idealized kinematic constraints (which can be regarded as infinitely stiff) and real free motion that can be measured (e.g by MOCAP) and inputted as kinematical driver.

An practical relevant example is the small displacements in human joints; these are impossible to measure accurately enough for motion specification during a MOCAP experiment, they are insignificant to the over-all dynamics of the gfroos motion (the motion of system inertia), but they are in some cases important for proper estimation of local effect such as moment arms of muscles and ligaments.

The “static” assumption does of course also hold true for actual static cases, such as analysis of static postures.
